## Why Anki Still Dominates Step 2 Prep

Step 2 CK is a different beast than Step 1. It's less about isolated biochemistry pathways and more about clinical decision-making — when to order a test, when to treat, when to refer. Some students assume Anki is only for Step 1 memorization, but that's a costly misconception.

The sheer **volume of clinical guidelines, drug choices, and diagnostic criteria** in Step 2 is enormous. Anki's spaced repetition algorithm ensures you review material right before you're about to forget it — which is exactly what you need when you're juggling 40+ organ systems of clinical medicine.

Beyond retention, Anki trains your brain to pattern-match on high-yield presentations, which is exactly what the NBME is testing. When a card forces you to answer "next best step in management," you're practicing the same cognitive skill the exam demands.

## How to Integrate Anki with Your Step 2 Study Schedule

### During Clerkships

The biggest mistake students make is saving Anki entirely for dedicated Step 2 prep. Instead, build the habit during third-year rotations. Here's a realistic daily schedule during clerkships:

- **30–50 new cards per day** from the relevant AnKing or Cheesy Dorian tags
- **Review due cards first** (never skip reviews — this is where the magic happens)
- Spend **20–30 minutes max** — consistency beats volume

Pair your daily Anki work with **UWorld question blocks** in the same subject. The synergy between seeing a concept in a question and then drilling it in Anki is significantly more powerful than either approach alone.

### During Dedicated Step 2 Prep

Once you hit dedicated, your Anki habits should already be established. Now you can:

- Increase new cards slightly (50–75/day if manageable)
- Focus on review pile maintenance — your due cards will spike if you've been consistent
- Add custom cards from **UWorld**, **Amboss**, or **Boards & Beyond** videos as needed

**Boards & Beyond** is especially useful here. Dr. Ryan's clinical medicine videos are concise and hit exactly the kind of conceptual framework that translates into solid Anki card material.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Downloading Everything and Using Nothing
More is not better. Pick **one primary deck** (AnKing Step 2 or Cheesy Dorian), commit to it, and supplement selectively. Fragmented review across five decks leads to review pile chaos.

### 2. Skipping Reviews to Do New Cards
This is the cardinal sin of Anki. Your due cards represent material you're about to forget. Prioritize reviews every single day, even if it means suspending new card additions temporarily.

### 3. Not Suspending Irrelevant Cards
The AnKing deck covers almost everything — including some obscure content that isn't worth your time. Use the tag system to **suspend cards** outside your current focus. You can always unsuspend them later.

### 4. Using Anki as a Substitute for Question Practice
Anki reinforces knowledge. **UWorld and Amboss** build clinical reasoning. You need both. A common mistake is spending 3 hours on Anki and skipping your UWorld block. For Step 2, question-based learning must remain central to your prep.
