## Why Cardiology Dominates Step 1

The cardiovascular system is deeply integrated with pathophysiology, pharmacology, and embryology — three pillars of the Step 1 exam. A single concept like heart failure can test you on Frank-Starling curves, diuretic mechanisms, and ACE inhibitor pharmacology all at once. The NBME loves this kind of cross-disciplinary testing, which means strong cardiology knowledge multiplies your gains across multiple question categories.

According to student reports and NBME content outlines, cardiology and cardiovascular pathophysiology represent roughly **10–15% of Step 1 content**, making it one of the single largest topic areas on the exam.

## The Core High-Yield Cardiology Topics

### Cardiac Physiology Fundamentals

Before you memorize a single disease, lock in the physiology. These concepts appear constantly — sometimes directly, sometimes as the foundation for a pharmacology or pathology question.

**Must-know physiology concepts:**
- Frank-Starling relationship and its clinical implications
- Cardiac output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
- Pressure-volume loops (normal, and in aortic stenosis/regurgitation, mitral stenosis/regurgitation)
- Wiggers diagram: be able to identify every phase and correlate with heart sounds
- Autonomic regulation of heart rate (baroreceptor reflex, vagal tone)

> **Pro tip:** Boards & Beyond has arguably the clearest cardiology physiology lectures available. Dr. Ryan's Wiggers diagram walkthrough alone is worth the subscription.

### Congenital Heart Defects

Congenital heart disease is extraordinarily high yield. The NBME tests it with clinical vignettes — a cyanotic infant, a child with a harsh murmur, an adult with recurrent paradoxical emboli. You need to know the anatomy, the shunt direction, and the downstream consequences.

**High-yield congenital lesions:**

| Defect | Shunt Direction | Key Feature | Associated Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| VSD | L→R (early) | Most common CHD | Eisenmenger if uncorrected |
| ASD | L→R | Wide fixed split S2 | Paradoxical emboli risk |
| PDA | L→R | Continuous "machine" murmur | Closed by indomethacin |
| Tetralogy of Fallot | R→L | Boot-shaped heart on CXR | Tet spells, squatting relief |
| Transposition of Great Vessels | Parallel circuits | Cyanosis at birth | Prostaglandin E1 keeps PDA open |
| Coarctation of the Aorta | N/A | Rib notching on CXR | Associated with bicuspid AV |
| Truncus Arteriosus | R→L | Single great vessel | Neural crest defect |

Eisenmenger syndrome deserves special attention — the NBME loves the concept of shunt reversal from chronic L→R overload causing pulmonary hypertension, eventually flipping to R→L with late-onset cyanosis.

### Valvular Heart Disease

Murmurs are tested heavily. You need to know the timing, location, quality, and maneuvers that affect each murmur.

**The highest-yield valvular conditions:**
- **Aortic Stenosis:** Crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur, radiates to carotids, pulsus parvus et tardus, syncope/angina/HF triad
- **Aortic Regurgitation:** Early diastolic decrescendo murmur, wide pulse pressure, Austin Flint murmur, bounding "water-hammer" pulse
- **Mitral Stenosis:** Opening snap + mid-diastolic rumble, LA enlargement, atrial fibrillation, dysphagia
- **Mitral Regurgitation:** Holosystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla, S3 gallop
- **MVP:** Mid-systolic click, moves earlier with Valsalva (decreased preload)
- **HOCM:** Harsh systolic murmur that **decreases** with increased preload (squatting) — the opposite of most murmurs

> **Mnemonics matter here.** Sketchy has a surprisingly effective cardiology section for pharmacology, but for murmur patterns, Anki/Anking decks with audio cards are unbeatable for building the "click" of instant recall.

### Cardiac Arrhythmias and EKG Interpretation

EKG questions show up regularly and reward students who practice. You don't need to be a cardiologist — you need to recognize a handful of patterns cold.

**Non-negotiable EKG findings:**
- **STEMI localization:** Which leads → which territory → which coronary artery
- **WPW:** Delta wave, short PR, wide QRS; risk of SVT and sudden death
- **Long QT:** Risk of Torsades de Pointes; caused by antiarrhythmics (quinidine, sotalol), antipsychotics, macrolides, hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia
- **Heart blocks:** 1st degree (prolonged PR), 2nd degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach — PR progressively lengthens), Mobitz II (sudden dropped beat — dangerous), 3rd degree (complete dissociation)
- **Atrial fibrillation:** Irregularly irregular, no P waves, CHA₂DS₂-VASc for anticoagulation

**STEMI localization cheat sheet:**
- Leads II, III, aVF → Inferior MI → RCA
- Leads V1–V4 → Anterior MI → LAD
- Leads I, aVL, V5–V6 → Lateral MI → LCx
- Posterior MI → Tall R in V1–V2, depression (reciprocal changes)

### Heart Failure Pathophysiology and Pharmacology

Heart failure questions on Step 1 are almost always pharmacology-integrated. You need to understand the hemodynamic rationale behind each drug class.

**Key HF pharmacology:**
- **ACE inhibitors/ARBs:** Reduce afterload and preload, decrease mortality, prevent ventricular remodeling
- **Beta-blockers (carvedilol, metoprolol, bisoprolol):** Reduce mortality in stable HFrEF
- **Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone):** Reduce mortality, especially post-MI with LV dysfunction
- **Loop diuretics (furosemide):** Symptomatic relief of volume overload — do NOT reduce mortality
- **Digoxin:** Increases contractility, reduces hospitalizations, no mortality benefit
- **Hydralazine + nitrates:** Used in African American patients or ACE inhibitor-intolerant patients

> **UWorld is non-negotiable for this section.** Their HF questions chain physiology → pharmacology → clinical decision-making beautifully. Do every cardiology question and read every explanation, even for ones you get right.

### Coronary Artery Disease and Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis is tested across multiple disciplines — biochemistry (lipid metabolism), pathology (foam cells, fatty streaks, fibrous plaques), and clinical medicine (angina, MI).

**High-yield pathology:**
- Stable angina: Fixed plaque >70% stenosis, ST depression during stress
- Unstable angina/NSTEMI: Plaque rupture with partial occlusion, elevated troponin in NSTEMI
- STEMI: Complete occlusion, ST elevation, time = myocardium
- Timeline of MI changes: Troponin rises 3–6 hrs (peaks 24 hrs), CK-MB peaks 24 hrs (good for reinfarction), LDH peaks 3–5 days
- Dressler syndrome: Autoimmune pericarditis 2–10 weeks post-MI

## How to Study Cardiology for Step 1: A Strategic Approach

### Build Your Foundation First

Don't jump into UWorld questions until you have a solid foundation. Use **Boards & Beyond** or **[Pathoma](https://www.amazon.com/Pathoma-Fundamentals-Pathology-Husain-Sattar/dp/0983224633?tag=uslmesite-20)** (chapters 9–10) for cardiovascular pathology, then reinforce with **[First Aid](https://www.amazon.com/First-Aid-USMLE-Step-2024/dp/1264946643?tag=uslmesite-20)** as your reference anchor. Read First Aid cardiology actively, not passively — annotate it as you go.

### Layer in Active Recall with Anki

The **AnKing deck** has comprehensive cardiology cards that align directly with First Aid and UWorld. These are particularly powerful for:
- Memorizing murmur characteristics and maneuver responses
- Locking in EKG patterns
- Drug mechanisms and side effects

Aim for 50–100 new cards per day during your dedicated study period, with consistent daily reviews.

### Use UWorld Strategically

Do cardiology questions in **timed, mixed blocks** after you've studied the content. Review your incorrect answers in a separate notebook or Anki card. The AMBOSS question bank is an excellent second-pass resource — their library explanations are more detailed than UWorld and often highlight mechanisms that Step 1 loves to test.

### Watch for Pharmacology Integration

Nearly every clinical cardiology question has a pharmacology hook. As you study each condition, actively ask: *What drugs are used? What are the mechanisms? What are the high-yield side effects?* For antiarrhythmics specifically, use the **Vaughan-Williams classification** as your framework:
- Class IA (quinidine, procainamide): Na⁺ channel blockers, prolong QT
- Class IB (lidocaine, mexiletine): Shorten AP, preferred post-MI
- Class IC (flecainide): Use-dependent Na⁺ block, avoid post-MI
- Class II (beta-blockers): AV node suppression
- Class III (amiodarone, sotalol): K⁺ channel block, prolong QT
- Class IV (verapamil, diltiazem): Ca²⁺ channel block, AV node

Amiodarone deserves a dedicated review — its side effect profile (pulmonary fibrosis, thyroid dysfunction, photosensitivity, hepatotoxicity, corneal deposits) is heavily tested.

## Common Cardiology Mistakes on Step 1

- **Confusing HOCM and aortic stenosis:** Both cause harsh systolic murmurs, but HOCM *decreases* with increased preload (squatting, leg raise) while AS doesn't change significantly.
- **Forgetting Eisenmenger:** Any chronic L→R shunt can eventually reverse. When you see late cyanosis in a patient with a known VSD/ASD history, think Eisenmenger.
- **Mixing up Mobitz I vs. II:** Mobitz II is the dangerous one requiring a pacemaker. The "irregular" Wenckebach (Mobitz I) is often benign.
- **Underestimating pericardial disease:** Cardiac tamponade (Beck's triad), constrictive pericarditis (Kussmaul sign, pericardial knock), and pericarditis (friction rub, saddle-shaped ST elevation) are frequently tested and often missed.
