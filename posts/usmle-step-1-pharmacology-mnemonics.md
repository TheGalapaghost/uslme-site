## Why Mnemonics Work for Step 1 Pharmacology

The human brain is notoriously bad at memorizing arbitrary lists but remarkably good at storing stories, patterns, and associations. That's the science behind why mnemonics outperform brute-force repetition for medical students.

Pharmacology demands you track:
- **Drug classes and mechanisms**
- **Clinical indications**
- **Side effect profiles**
- **Key contraindications**
- **Toxicity and antidotes**

Across hundreds of drugs. No one does this cleanly with flashcards alone. The best-performing students combine mnemonics with **spaced repetition** (Anki/Anking decks are the gold standard here) and visual storytelling tools like **Sketchy Pharm**, all anchored to **[First Aid for the USMLE Step 1](https://www.amazon.com/First-Aid-USMLE-Step-2024/dp/1264946643?tag=uslmesite-20)**, which essentially turns entire drug classes into mnemonic-rich visual scenes you won't forget.

## Top High-Yield Pharmacology Mnemonics by Category

### Autonomic Pharmacology

This is usually the first major pharmacology block and the one with the most overlapping mechanisms. Master this early and everything else builds on it.

**Cholinergic Effects — "DUMBELS" (Muscarinic Toxicity)**
> **D**efecation, **U**rination, **M**iosis, **B**radycardia, **E**mesis, **L**acrimation, **S**alivation

This is essential for organophosphate poisoning questions, which appear regularly on Step 1. The antidote is atropine (plus pralidoxime if early).

**Anticholinergic Side Effects — "Can't See, Can't Spit, Can't Pee, Can't Poop, Hot as a Hare, Red as a Beet, Mad as a Hatter"**

Classic boards presentation: elderly patient with urinary retention, dry mouth, blurry vision, and confusion after starting a new medication. Think: tricyclic antidepressants, antihistamines, antipsychotics, or oxybutynin.

**Beta-Blocker Cardioselectivity — "B1 = 1 Heart, B2 = 2 Lungs"**

Cardioselective beta-1 blockers (metoprolol, atenolol, esmolol, acebutolol, bisoprolol) are preferred in patients with asthma or COPD. Remember: **MAABE** (Metoprolol, Atenolol, Acebutolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol).

### Antibiotics and Antimicrobials

Antibiotic pharmacology is a major Step 1 focus — especially mechanisms of resistance and spectrum of coverage.

**Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors — "Very Penicillin-Like Carbapenems Overwhelm Bacteria"**
> Vancomycin, Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Carbapenems, Monobactams (Aztreonam)

**Protein Synthesis Inhibitors — "Buy AT 30, CELL at 50"**
> **30S inhibitors:** Aminoglycosides, Tetracyclines  
> **50S inhibitors:** Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin (macrolides), Lincomycin (clindamycin), Linezolid

This is one of the most tested mnemonics on the entire Step 1 exam. Every year there are multiple mechanism-of-action questions that require knowing which ribosomal subunit a drug targets.

**Bacteriostatic vs. Bactericidal — "Static = CSTET"**
> **C**hloramphenicol, **S**ulfonamides, **T**etracyclines, **E**rythromycin (macrolides), **T**rimethoprim

Everything else in common use is bactericidal. This matters clinically because bactericidal agents are preferred in immunocompromised patients and serious infections like endocarditis.

### Cardiovascular Pharmacology

**Antiarrhythmic Classes (Vaughan-Williams) — "Some Block Potassium Channels"**
> **I** = Sodium channel blockers (lidocaine, flecainide, quinidine)  
> **II** = Beta-blockers  
> **III** = Potassium channel blockers (amiodarone, sotalol, ibutilide, dofetilide)  
> **IV** = Calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem)

For Class IA agents specifically: **"The Queen Produces Disopyramide"** → Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide. These prolong both the QRS and QT intervals.

**Amiodarone Side Effects — "P-HALT"**
> **P**ulmonary fibrosis, **H**epatotoxicity, **A**rrhythmia (paradoxically), **L**ipofuscin deposits (skin), **T**hyroid dysfunction (hypo or hyper)

Amiodarone has the longest list of side effects of any antiarrhythmic and shows up in clinical vignettes constantly. Boards love asking why you'd avoid it long-term.

---

### Diuretics

**Diuretic Sites of Action — "Glomerulus, PCT, Loop, DCT, Collecting Duct"**

| Diuretic Class | Site of Action | Key Drug(s) | Key Side Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Osmotic | Proximal tubule/Loop | Mannitol | Pulmonary edema (↑ ECF initially) |
| Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors | PCT | Acetazolamide | Metabolic acidosis |
| Loop Diuretics | Thick ascending Loop of Henle | Furosemide, Bumetanide | Hypokalemia, ototoxicity |
| Thiazides | Early DCT | Hydrochlorothiazide | Hypokalemia, hypercalcemia |
| Potassium-Sparing | Collecting duct | Spironolactone, Triamterene | Hyperkalemia |

**Furosemide Side Effects — "OH DANG"**
> **O**totoxicity, **H**ypokalemia, **D**ehydration, **A**llergy (sulfa), **N**ephritis (interstitial), **G**out (hyperuricemia)

### Oncology Pharmacology

Chemotherapy mechanisms are extremely high-yield, especially for Step 1.

**Cell Cycle Phase-Specific vs. Non-Specific**
- **Phase-specific (M phase):** Vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine), Taxanes (paclitaxel)
- **Phase-specific (S phase):** Methotrexate, 5-FU, Cytarabine, Hydroxyurea
- **Phase non-specific:** Alkylating agents, Nitrosoureas, Platinum agents, Antibiotics (doxorubicin)

**Vincristine vs. Vinblastine — "Vincristine = Cranial Nerves, Vinblastine = Bone Marrow Blast"**
> Vincristine causes neurotoxicity (peripheral neuropathy, paralytic ileus)  
> Vinblastine causes bone marrow suppression (think "B" for Bone)

**Alkylating Agent Toxicity — "BCNU = Bloody CNS"** (carmustine crosses the blood-brain barrier — used in brain tumors)

### Psychiatric Pharmacology

**SSRI Side Effects — "SSRI = Sexual dysfunction, Serotonin syndrome risk, GI distress, Insomnia"**

The boards frequently test serotonin syndrome: **"HEAT" — Hyperthermia, Encephalopathy (clonus, agitation), Autonomic instability, Tremor/Tachycardia.** Distinguish from neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS), which features lead-pipe rigidity and follows antipsychotic use.

**Lithium Toxicity — "THIRST"**
> **T**remor (fine → coarse with toxicity), **H**ypothyroidism, **I**nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, **R**enal tubular acidosis risk, **S**eizures (severe toxicity), **T**eratogen (Ebstein anomaly)

Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index. NSAIDs and thiazides increase lithium levels by reducing renal clearance — a classic drug interaction question.

## How to Actually Use Mnemonics Effectively

Knowing a mnemonic and being able to retrieve it under exam pressure are two very different things. Here's how to make them stick:

### 1. Attach Emotion and Story
The reason Sketchy works is that it embeds mnemonics into bizarre, emotionally engaging stories. When you create your own mnemonics, make them weird, funny, or personal — neutral mnemonics fade faster.

### 2. Practice Active Recall from Day One
Don't just re-read your mnemonics. Cover them and force your brain to generate the information. This is the core principle behind Anki's algorithm and why passive review consistently underperforms in retention studies.

### 3. Layer Mnemonics with UWorld Questions
Every time you encounter a drug in UWorld, link it back to its mnemonic. High-yield pharmacology topics — including diuretics, antibiotics, antiarrhythmics, and psychiatric medications — appear in clinical vignettes that test whether you can *apply* the mnemonic, not just recite it.

### 4. Review Regularly, Not Cram-Style
Space your mnemonic reviews. A drug mechanism reviewed once during dedicated study and never again will not survive to test day. Build pharmacy review into your Anki daily cards so side effects and mechanisms stay fresh.
